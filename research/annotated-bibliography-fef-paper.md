## How to Use This Document

Each entry has four parts:
- **What it is** — one sentence on the work itself
- **Why it matters for your paper** — specific connection to your argument
- **What to absorb** — the key ideas, terms, or moves you need to internalize
- **Effort rating** — honest time estimate and depth of engagement required

**Effort ratings:**
- 🟢 **Skim** — Read introduction + conclusion + flip through. 1–2 hours. You need to know it exists and be able to cite it accurately.
- 🟡 **Read selectively** — Specific chapters or sections noted. 3–6 hours. You need to understand the argument well enough to engage with it in a footnote or paragraph.
- 🔴 **Read carefully** — Full read, take notes, internalize the method. 8–15 hours. These are the works your paper is in direct conversation with.

The goal is not mastery of everything. It is knowing which works you are building on, which you are responding to, and which you merely need to acknowledge exist.

### A1. Welch, John W. "Chiasmus in the Book of Mormon." *BYU Studies* 10, no. 1 (1969): 69–84.

**What it is:** The foundational paper introducing chiasmus as a structural feature of the Book of Mormon. Welch identified inverted parallel structures in BofM passages and argued they reflect Hebrew literary conventions.

**Why it matters for your paper:** This is the closest structural predecessor to your work. Welch did for chiasmus what you are doing for FEFs — identified a recurring structural pattern, gave it a name, showed it appears consistently, and noted its parallel to ancient conventions. Your paper should acknowledge this lineage explicitly. The "independent discovery" framing you use for the Hebrew parallel echoes Welch's argument, and reviewers will notice that either favorably or critically depending on how you handle it.

**What to absorb:**
- How Welch defines the pattern formally (the defining criteria)
- How he presents examples before generalizing
- The hedged language he uses when discussing implications
- The structural move: phenomenon → definition → examples → implications

**Effort:** 🔴 **Read carefully.** This is short (15 pages) and foundational. Read it twice — once for content, once for structure. Notice every methodological move he makes.

### A2. Welch, John W., ed. *Chiasmus in Antiquity: Structures, Analyses, Exegesis.* Hildesheim: Gerstenberg, 1981.

**What it is:** A collection of essays applying chiastic analysis to texts across multiple ancient traditions — Hebrew Bible, New Testament, Greek, Ugaritic, and others.

**Why it matters for your paper:** Establishes that chiasmus is a cross-cultural ancient literary phenomenon, not a quirk of one tradition. Relevant because your Hebrew parallel section makes an analogous argument — FEFs parallel Hebrew narrative conventions, and the parallelism is meaningful regardless of how one reads BofM origins.

**What to absorb:**
- The introductory essay on chiasmus across traditions
- The methodological discussion of what counts as genuine chiasmus vs. coincidental inversion
- The criteria questions: how do you know a pattern is real rather than imposed by the analyst?

**Effort:** 🟡 **Read selectively.** Read Welch's introduction and one or two essays on Hebrew Bible chiasmus. Skip the sections on Greek and Ugaritic unless you find them genuinely interesting. 3–4 hours.

### A3. Roper, Matthew, Paul J. Fields, and G. Bruce Schaalje. "Stylometric Analyses of the Book of Mormon: A Short History." *Journal of Book of Mormon Studies* 21 (2012): 28–45.

**What it is:** A review and defense of stylometric approaches to BofM authorship attribution, summarizing the Hilton, Larsen, and other wordprint studies and responding to critiques.

**Why it matters for your paper:** This is the direct predecessor in the stylometric conversation. Your paper proposes colometric stylometry as a *new channel* independent of the lexical stylometry these authors do. You need to understand their method well enough to explain why yours is different and why "different channel" matters — independent methods converging on the same clustering is stronger evidence than one method alone.

**What to absorb:**
- How they define "wordprint" and what features they measure
- The key methodological objection: critics argue BofM passages attributed to different authors aren't long enough for reliable stylometric analysis
- Their response to that objection
- The clustering results: which attributed authors group together vs. separately

**Effort:** 🔴 **Read carefully.** This is the paper you are most directly extending. 2–3 hours for this specific paper, but worth it.

### A4. Hilton, John L. "On Verifying Wordprint Studies: Book of Mormon Authorship." *BYU Studies* 30, no. 3 (1990): 89–108.

**What it is:** One of the earlier and more methodologically careful wordprint studies, introducing the noncontextual word pattern approach to BofM authorship.

**Why it matters for your paper:** Hilton's work is the foundation of the positive stylometric case. Your paper doesn't need to adjudicate whether his findings are correct, but it needs to position itself: "Previous work has identified authorial differences through lexical/wordprint analysis. The present study identifies analogous differences through a structurally independent channel — colometric architecture."

**What to absorb:**
- The concept of "noncontextual words" (function words that appear regardless of topic)
- Why he argues these are better authorship indicators than content words
- The basic finding: BofM authors cluster separately from each other and from Joseph Smith

**Effort:** 🟡 **Read selectively.** Introduction and conclusions carefully; skim the statistical sections unless you want to go deep on the methodology. 2 hours.

### A5. Hardy, Grant. *Understanding the Book of Mormon: A Reader's Guide.* New York: Oxford University Press, 2010.

**What it is:** The most sophisticated literary-critical reading of the Book of Mormon in print. Hardy treats the text as a complex literary artifact, analyzing narrative structure, perspective, and authorial voice across the attributed authors (Nephi, Mormon, Moroni).

**Why it matters for your paper:** Hardy is doing literary analysis of exactly the authorial sections you are doing structural analysis of. His characterizations of Mormon's editorial voice, Nephi's narrative method, and the structural differences between sections are your closest analogue in the existing literature. Your FEF findings should either confirm or complicate what Hardy finds through literary reading. If they confirm it, you have convergent evidence from two independent methods. If they complicate it, that's an interesting finding worth discussing.

**What to absorb:**
- Hardy's characterization of Mormon as editor/narrator — specifically the passages where he discusses how Mormon frames and organizes his abridgment
- The discussion of Nephi's two voices (narrative vs. doctrinal)
- Hardy's treatment of how the different attributed authors "sound" different — his intuitions about this are the literary equivalent of what you're trying to quantify
- The concept of "implied author" — Hardy uses this from narratology and it's useful for your argument

**Effort:** 🔴 **Read carefully, but strategically.** This is a full book and it rewards full reading eventually, but for the paper: read the introduction and the chapters on Nephi and Mormon carefully. Skim the Moroni chapter. Total: 6–8 hours focused reading. This is probably the single most important book on your list for deepening your argument.

### B1. Alter, Robert. *The Art of Biblical Narrative.* New York: Basic Books, 1981.

**What it is:** The book that essentially founded literary-critical biblical studies in the English-speaking world. Alter demonstrates that Hebrew narrative has sophisticated literary artistry — recurring conventions, type-scenes, patterns of repetition — that can be analyzed using tools from literary criticism.

**Why it matters for your paper:** This is your structural and prose model. Alter is doing for Hebrew narrative what you are doing for Book of Mormon narrative — identifying recurring structural patterns, naming them, showing they are consistent, drawing literary conclusions. His method (demonstrate by accumulation of examples, introduce the term after the reader is already convinced) is exactly what your paper should do. Also: his concept of "type-scene" is a direct analogue to FEF. He defined a type-scene as a conventional narrative unit that appears in recognizable form at key structural moments. FEFs are arguably your version of a type-scene.

**What to absorb:**
- The introduction: Alter's case for literary reading of biblical narrative
- Chapter 1: "A Literary Approach to the Bible" — his methodological justification
- Chapter 3: "Biblical Type-Scenes and the Uses of Convention" — this is your closest analogue; read it carefully
- His prose style throughout: notice how he integrates close reading of specific verses with broader structural claims, and how he hedges

**Effort:** 🔴 **Read carefully (selected chapters).** Introduction + chapters 1 and 3 are essential. 4–5 hours. Return to other chapters as your argument develops. This book will also simply make you a better reader of the kind of texts you're working with.

---

### B2. Kugel, James L. *The Idea of Biblical Poetry: Parallelism and Its History.* New Haven: Yale University Press, 1981.

**What it is:** A major revision of how scholars think about Hebrew parallelism. Kugel argues that the traditional view (biblical poetry = A-line + B-line where B restates A) is too simple — the B-line always does something *more* than restate, it advances or intensifies the A-line.

**Why it matters for your paper:** Kugel's B-line analysis is relevant to your "escalation earns its own line" principle in your colometry. His argument that the second element of a parallel always does more than the first is a theoretical grounding for why you split lines where you do. Also: the parallelism tradition is the broader scholarly context your colometric method sits within, and Kugel is the most cited figure in that conversation.

**What to absorb:**
- Part 1: Kugel's critique of Lowth and the traditional view of Hebrew poetry
- The core insight: "A, and what's more, B" — the B-line intensifies rather than merely restates
- The implications for where you break lines: a restatement that intensifies earns separate treatment from one that merely echoes

**Effort:** 🟡 **Read selectively.** Part 1 (roughly the first 100 pages) is essential for your purposes. The historical survey in Part 2 is interesting but not necessary for the paper. 4 hours.

### B3. Berlin, Adele. *Poetics and Interpretation of Biblical Narrative.* Sheffield: Almond Press, 1983.

**What it is:** A short, lucid application of narratological and linguistic theory (Jakobson, Russian Formalism) to Hebrew narrative. Berlin argues for a poetics approach — studying the literary conventions and devices of the text as a system.

**Why it matters for your paper:** Berlin's approach to "poetics" is the theoretical umbrella that covers what you're doing. You are developing a poetics of Book of Mormon narrative — identifying its conventions (FEFs, expansion lines, framing devices) as a system. Her book shows how to do this rigorously without being reductive.

**What to absorb:**
- Her introduction: the case for poetics as a methodology
- The distinction between "story" and "discourse" (what happens vs. how it's told) — this maps onto the distinction between BofM content and BofM structure that your paper is exploiting
- Her treatment of "repetition" as a structural device (relevant to wayyehi/AICTP frequency)

**Effort:** 🟢 **Skim.** This is short (170 pages) and dense. Read the introduction carefully and skim the rest. 2–3 hours. Enough to cite accurately and borrow the "poetics" framing if you want it.

### B4. Fokkelman, J. P. *Narrative Art in Genesis.* Assen: Van Gorcum, 1975.

**What it is:** Fokkelman's structural analysis of Hebrew narrative at the micro level — clause by clause, line by line. He identifies compositional units, chiastic structures, and narrative "scenes" with extraordinary precision.

**Why it matters for your paper:** Fokkelman is the closest model for what you are doing at the line level. His analysis of how Hebrew narrative organizes information within a scene — framing clause, narrative body, closing formula — is the scholarly tradition your FEF definition is implicitly working within. The "wayyiqtol + circumstantial protasis" construction you identify as the Hebrew parallel to FEFs is exactly the kind of thing Fokkelman maps.

**What to absorb:**
- His method of structural analysis: how he identifies the boundaries of compositional units
- The treatment of temporal/circumstantial clauses as "scene-setting" frames (directly relevant to your FEF definition)
- His notation system (you don't need to adopt it, but understanding it helps you read his analyses)

**Effort:** 🟡 **Read selectively.** Read one extended analysis of a Genesis passage in detail — maybe the Joseph story (chapters 37–50). See how he handles the framing constructions. 3–4 hours.

### B5. Muilenburg, James. "Form Criticism and Beyond." *Journal of Biblical Literature* 88, no. 1 (1969): 1–18.

**What it is:** Muilenburg's presidential address to the Society of Biblical Literature, founding "rhetorical criticism" as a method. He argues that form criticism (studying generic conventions) misses the artistry of individual texts — that each text has a unique rhetorical shape that must be described on its own terms.

**Why it matters for your paper:** Muilenburg's move — from generic patterns to individual compositional artistry — is analogous to what you're doing. You're not just saying "FEFs are a type"; you're saying "different authors use FEFs differently, and that variation is itself meaningful." Also: his paper is short, famous, and frequently cited in biblical studies methodology discussions. Knowing it signals scholarly literacy to reviewers.

**What to absorb:**
- The core argument: studying a text's generic form is not enough; you must also study its specific rhetorical realization
- The concept of "rhetorical criticism" as attending to how a text does what it does, not just what it does
- The methodological move from "this text belongs to genre X" to "this text uses the conventions of genre X in this particular way"

**Effort:** 🟢 **Skim / read once.** It's 18 pages. Read it in one sitting. 1 hour. More important for what it represents than for complex argumentation.

### B6. Watson, Wilfred G. E. *Classical Hebrew Poetry: A Guide to Its Techniques.* Sheffield: JSOT Press, 1984.

**What it is:** A comprehensive technical reference for Hebrew poetic devices — parallelism, chiasmus, meter, word-pairs, repetition, strophic structure.

**Why it matters for your paper:** This is a reference work, not a reading. When you need to describe a specific Hebrew structural feature accurately — like the wayyiqtol + protasis construction — Watson is where you check the technical terminology and confirm you're describing it correctly.

**What to absorb:**
- The sections on parallelism and repetition (most relevant to your argument)
- The treatment of "initial" and "terminal" positions in verse — how Hebrew poetry privileges the opening and closing positions of a unit (relevant to why FEFs appear at verse openings)

**Effort:** 🟢 **Reference only.** Don't read this cover to cover. Treat it as a dictionary. Look up specific devices when you need to describe them precisely. 0 hours upfront; use as needed during drafting.

### B7. Berlin, Adele. *The Dynamics of Biblical Parallelism.* Indiana University Press, 1985. Rev. ed. 2008.

**What it is:** A linguistic analysis of how Hebrew parallelism actually works — phonologically, lexically, and syntactically. Distinct from Berlin's *Poetics and Interpretation of Biblical Narrative* (B3 above), which is about narrative rather than poetry.

**Why it matters for your paper:** This is more directly relevant to your line-breaking methodology than the narrative poetics book. Berlin provides the linguistic "rules" of how parallelism functions — why certain pairs belong together, what makes a second line intensify rather than merely repeat. Your editorial decisions about when to stack parallels vertically and when to merge are implicitly applying Berlin's framework. Being able to cite the linguistic basis for your break decisions strengthens the methodology section.

**What to absorb:**
- The three levels of parallelism: phonological (sound), lexical (word pairs), syntactic (grammatical structure)
- How syntactic parallelism creates the structural expectation that governs where a reader/listener processes a line break
- Her critique of Lowth's simplistic three-category system — the reality is more nuanced than "synonymous/antithetical/synthetic"

**Effort:** 🟡 **Read selectively.** Introduction + the chapter on syntactic parallelism. 3–4 hours. More linguistically rigorous than Alter or Kugel, which is exactly what a reviewer looking at your methodology would want to see cited.

### B8. Dobbs-Allsopp, F. W. *On Biblical Poetry.* Oxford University Press, 2015.

**What it is:** The current scholarly state-of-the-art on Hebrew poetry, moving beyond the Alter/Kugel debate. Dobbs-Allsopp argues against the search for rigid meter and instead describes biblical poetry as a form of free verse, with the *line* (not the couplet) as the fundamental unit.

**Why it matters for your paper:** This may be the single most theoretically important work for your methodology, because Dobbs-Allsopp is arguing exactly what your colometric practice assumes — that the line is the primary unit of Hebrew poetic/rhetorical organization, and that rhythm emerges from lineation rather than from imposed metrical patterns. His focus on orality and performance-based form also connects directly to your foundational test (atomic breath unit). If a reviewer asks "what theoretical basis do you have for treating individual sense-lines as the unit of analysis?" — Dobbs-Allsopp is the answer.

**What to absorb:**
- The argument for the line as fundamental unit (not the couplet or the verse)
- The concept of rhythm without meter — cadence emerging from natural speech units
- The orality dimension: how poetic forms reflect performance contexts
- Why the search for Greco-Roman-style meter in Hebrew has failed, and what replaces it

**Effort:** 🟡 **Read selectively.** Introduction + the chapters on the line and on rhythm. 4–5 hours. This is dense but directly foundational for your theoretical framework. Worth the investment.

## SECTION C — Stylometry and Computational Authorship

*These establish the broader methodological conversation. You need to know this field exists and be able to describe your method's relationship to it.*

---

### C1. Mosteller, Frederick, and David L. Wallace. *Inference and Disputed Authorship: The Federalist.* Reading, MA: Addison-Wesley, 1964.

**What it is:** The founding study of computational authorship attribution, using function word frequencies to determine whether Madison or Hamilton wrote disputed Federalist Papers.

**Why it matters for your paper:** This is the origin point of the entire stylometric tradition. Citing it locates your work in a 60-year scholarly conversation. More importantly: Mosteller and Wallace used *structural* features (function word frequency) rather than content features, which is the same logic you're applying — structural/architectural features of text reveal authorship independent of what the text is about.

**What to absorb:**
- The core methodological insight: content words vary with topic; structural/function words are topic-independent and more reliably authorial
- The basic approach: measure features, cluster by similarity, see if clustering matches attribution
- The limitations they acknowledge: sample size, the "closed set" problem (you can only attribute to known candidates)

**Effort:** 🟢 **Skim the introduction and conclusion.** The statistical apparatus is deep and you don't need it. 1–2 hours.

---

### C2. Burrows, John. "'Delta': A Measure of Stylistic Difference and a Guide to Likely Authorship." *Literary and Linguistic Computing* 17, no. 3 (2002): 267–287.

**What it is:** The paper introducing "Delta," which became the standard distance measure in computational stylometry. Delta measures the distance between texts based on the frequency profiles of the most common words.

**Why it matters for your paper:** Delta is the current dominant method in computational stylometry. If you're writing for a DH venue, reviewers will ask why you didn't use Delta or a Delta-like measure. Your answer is that Delta measures *lexical* features (word frequencies), whereas you're measuring *structural* features (line-length distributions, FEF frequency) — these are orthogonal channels and that independence is part of the point.

**What to absorb:**
- What Delta measures and how it works (at a conceptual level, not the math)
- The distinction between Delta (most common words) and other approaches (content words, function words, syntactic features)
- The implicit assumption: that frequency of common words reflects authorial habit rather than topic — and why structural features like FEF frequency make the same assumption

**Effort:** 🟢 **Skim.** Read the abstract, introduction, and conclusion. 1 hour.

---

### C3. Koppel, Moshe, Jonathan Schler, and Shlomo Argamon. "Computational Methods in Authorship Attribution." *Journal of the American Society for Information Science and Technology* 60, no. 1 (2009): 9–26.

**What it is:** A review article surveying the state of computational authorship attribution methods as of the late 2000s.

**Why it matters for your paper:** This is the survey article you cite when you say "previous computational authorship work has focused on lexical and syntactic features." It gives you a single citable source that covers the field rather than requiring you to cite a dozen individual papers.

**What to absorb:**
- The taxonomy of features used in authorship attribution: lexical, character-level, syntactic, semantic
- The absence of *structural/architectural* features in the taxonomy — this is your opening. Nobody in the computational stylometry tradition has used colometric structure as a feature set.
- The standard evaluation framework: what counts as evidence for authorship attribution

**Effort:** 🟢 **Skim.** Abstract, introduction, taxonomy section, conclusion. 1–2 hours.

## SECTION D — Hebrew Narrative Grammar

*These give you the technical grounding for the Hebrew parallel section. You need enough to describe the wayyiqtol + protasis construction accurately; you don't need to become a Hebrew grammarian.*

### D1. Waltke, Bruce K., and M. O'Connor. *An Introduction to Biblical Hebrew Syntax.* Winona Lake, IN: Eisenbrauns, 1990.

**What it is:** The standard English-language reference grammar of Biblical Hebrew.

**Why it matters for your paper:** When you describe the Hebrew narrative backbone (wayyiqtol + temporal/circumstantial protasis) in Section VI, you need to describe it accurately and with correct terminology. This is your reference for that.

**What to absorb:**
- The section on wayyiqtol (the narrative past tense — the Hebrew "backbone" of narrative)
- The section on circumstantial clauses (the participial clauses that create the "framing" protasis)
- The concept of "clause hierarchy" — how Hebrew narrative organizes clauses into main-line and off-line (backgrounded) material

**Effort:** 🟢 **Reference only.** Find the relevant sections (the index is good), read those sections, take notes on the terminology. 2 hours of targeted reading.

---

### D2. Longacre, Robert E. *Joseph: A Story of Divine Providence.* Winona Lake, IN: Eisenbrauns, 1989.

**What it is:** A full discourse analysis of the Joseph narrative using text-linguistic methodology, mapping the narrative's clause types, topical prominence, and structural organization.

**Why it matters for your paper:** Longacre's discourse analysis framework — particularly his distinction between "mainline" narrative clauses (wayyiqtol) and "off-line" clauses (circumstantials, participials, nominals) — is the technical background for your claim that FEFs function as "mainline + scene-setting protasis" units in Hebrew narrative.

**What to absorb:**
- The introduction to his discourse analysis method
- The distinction between foreground (mainline narrative) and background (off-line) clauses
- How scene-setting temporal/circumstantial clauses function as a unit with the mainline clause that follows them — this is the Hebrew parallel to FEF

**Effort:** 🟡 **Read selectively.** Introduction and the methodological chapters. 3–4 hours.

---

### D3. Bandstra, Barry L. "The Syntax of Particle *KY* in Biblical Hebrew and Ugaritic." PhD dissertation, Yale University, 1982.

**What it is:** Bandstra's work on Hebrew discourse particles — the words that mark logical and rhetorical relationships between clauses.

**Why it matters for your paper:** Discourse markers in Hebrew are the Hebrew equivalents of "For behold," "And now," "Behold" in your FEF definition. If you're going to argue that BofM framing devices parallel Hebrew discourse markers, you need to know what the Hebrew markers actually do.

**What to absorb:**
- The function of *hinneh* ("behold") as a presentative/deictic marker
- The function of *we'atta* ("and now") as a discourse pivot
- The general concept of "discourse marker" as a word whose primary function is structural/relational rather than referential

**Effort:** 🟢 **Skim / reference.** Find a more accessible article on Hebrew discourse markers if the dissertation isn't available. 2 hours.
