# 12 — Grammatical Glossary

A reference of grammatical and structural terms used in the BOM Reader colometric editorial methodology. Each entry has: the term, a plain-English definition, how it functions in our line-breaking decisions, and a corpus example.

---

## Clause-Level Terms

### Atomic Thought
A unit of meaning that a reader can process without needing the next line to resolve it. The foundational test for every line break: can this line stand alone as a complete idea?
- **Colometric use:** Every line must pass this test or be merged with its neighbor.
- **Example:** "And he will take upon him death," — complete thought. vs. "And he will take upon him" — incomplete without its object.

### Atomic Breath Unit
A line that can be delivered in one natural breath at reading pace. The secondary test after the atomic thought test.
- **Colometric use:** When the atomic thought test is ambiguous, the breath test can decide.
- **Example:** "through the blood of the Lamb." — one breath. Not in tension with being an atomic thought.

### Main Clause
The core subject + verb construction that carries the primary assertion of a sentence. Other clauses depend on it.
- **Colometric use:** FEF frames suspend resolution until the main clause arrives. The main verb typically begins a new line after the frame completes.
- **Example:** In "And it came to pass that after Alma had spoken, / **the people began to be astonished**" — the bolded text is the main clause.

### Subordinate Clause
A clause that depends on the main clause — introduced by "that," "which," "who," "when," "after," "because," "if," etc. Cannot stand alone as a sentence.
- **Colometric use:** Subordinate clauses often break from the main clause at their introducer ("that," "because," "when"), but only if both halves pass the atomic thought test.
- **Example:** "that he may loose the bands of death / **which bind his people**" — the "which" clause is subordinate.

### Content Clause (Complement Clause)
A "that" clause functioning as the object of a verb of speaking, thinking, or perceiving — it supplies WHAT was said/thought/seen.
- **Colometric use:** Breaks from its verb when the verb is complete without it (Rule 3): "he hath said / that no unclean thing can inherit the kingdom."
- **Example:** "and he testified / that the things which he saw and heard..."

### Purpose Clause
A clause expressing WHY or FOR WHAT PURPOSE something was done — introduced by "that [subject] might/may/should."
- **Colometric use:** Purpose clauses break (Rule 7): "that they might destroy Alma and Amulek."
- **Example:** "he sent forth his armies / that they might destroy them."

### Causal Clause
A clause expressing WHY something happened — introduced by "because," "for," "since."
- **Colometric use:** Causal clauses break (Rule 6): "because they had hardened their hearts."
- **Example:** "for the multitude was great, / and he spake on this wise:"

### Result Clause
A clause expressing the OUTCOME — introduced by "insomuch that," "so that," "then."
- **Colometric use:** "Then" after a conditional breaks from what precedes it: "insomuch that it has not been found in us, / then will our state be awful."

### Conditional Clause
A clause expressing a condition — introduced by "if," "except," "unless."
- **Colometric use:** Conditionals typically break, creating the if/then structure visible across lines.
- **Example:** "if ye will repent, / and harden not your hearts, / then will I have mercy upon you."

## Phrase-Level Terms

### Manner Adverbial
A prepositional phrase answering HOW an action was done. Modifies the verb.
- **Colometric use:** Manner adverbials merge with their verb (Rule 21): "spoke unto them, according to his word" stays on one line.
- **Example:** "gave a genealogy of his fathers, according to his memory."

### Source/Authority Phrase
A prepositional phrase identifying BY WHAT POWER or FROM WHAT SOURCE an action occurs — "according to the Spirit," "through the blood of the Lamb," "by the power of God."
- **Colometric use:** Source/authority phrases split from their verb (Rule 21) because they make an independent theological claim.
- **Example:** "their garments were washed white / through the blood of the Lamb."

### Circumstantial Frame
A phrase or clause that sets the scene — temporal, spatial, or situational background before the main action.
- **Colometric use:** Circumstantial frames attach to their content (Rule 8) but may earn their own line when they set a scene that the reader needs to process before the action.
- **Example:** "and in this awful state / we shall not dare to look up to our God."

### Participial Clause
A clause built on a participle (-ing form or past participle) rather than a finite verb. Functions as a modifier.
- **Colometric use:** Participial clauses that describe a distinct state or action earn their own line: "knowing even as we know now."
- **Example:** "she being a virgin, / a precious and chosen vessel, / who shall be overshadowed."

### Genitive Phrase
A phrase expressing possession or relationship — "of the Lord," "of the people," "of their fathers." Follows a noun to identify or specify it.
- **Colometric use:** A genitive noun separated from its "of..." complement is unidentifiable and must merge. "The will" without "of God" fails the atomic thought test.
- **Example:** "according to the will and power and deliverance of Jesus Christ."

## Structural Terms

### Front-End Frame (FEF)
A structurally irreducible opening construction that suspends resolution until a main verb arrives. The line cannot be broken further without creating an incomplete thought. "And it came to pass that" is the most common FEF, but the category includes genitive chains, temporal frames, and participial preambles.
- **Colometric use:** FEF frames complete on one line (or across multiple lines if very long), with the main verb beginning the next line.
- **Example:** "And now it came to pass that after I, Nephi, having heard all the words of my father," — the entire line is an irreducible FEF frame.

### AICTP (And It Came To Pass)
The most frequent FEF in the BofM — the wayyehi formula. Functions as a narrative frame signaling "something is about to happen." Always anticipatory.
- **Colometric use:** "And it came to pass that" stays on one line (Rule 1). When followed by a temporal clause, the temporal reference completes on the same line; the main verb begins the next.
- **Example:** "And it came to pass that in the commencement of the seventeenth year of the reign of the judges, / there was continual peace."

### Speech Attribution Formula
A construction introducing quoted speech — "he said unto them:" / "Alma answered and said:" / "the Lord spake, saying:"
- **Colometric use:** The attribution goes on its own line; the quoted content begins on the next line. Exception: when the quoted content is a single short word (Yea, Nay), it merges with the attribution.
- **Example:** "And he said: / Thus saith the Lord God--"

### Escalatory Appositive
An appositional phrase that escalates in theological register from what precedes it — each successive appositive adds a higher-order attribute. Coined term for this project.
- **Colometric use:** Escalatory appositives stack vertically (Rule 14/20). Each step up in register earns its own line.
- **Example:** "she being a virgin, / a precious and chosen vessel, / who shall be overshadowed / and conceive by the power of the Holy Ghost."

### Narrowing Vocative
A compound vocative address where the speaker names a broad group, then narrows to a specific subset.
- **Colometric use:** The narrowing point earns a break (Rule 23). The BofM consistently moves broad → narrow, never the reverse.
- **Example:** "O ye wicked and perverse generation, / ye lawyers and hypocrites."

### Definitional "Which Is" Clause
A relative clause that makes a theological equation or reveals what something IS — not merely describing an attribute.
- **Colometric use:** Definitional clauses break (Rule 24) when they equate or reveal. Descriptive attribute clauses merge.
- **Example (break):** "even that same judgment of which we have spoken, / which is the end." — equation.
- **Example (merge):** "a life which is endless" — attribute.

### Editorial Narrator Formula
A construction where Mormon (the narrator) steps back from the story to address the reader directly — most commonly "And thus we see that..."
- **Colometric use:** "And thus we see" stands on its own line; "that [lesson]" begins the next (Rule 25). This is Mormon's signature compositional marker.
- **Example:** "And thus we see / that the Spirit of the Lord began to withdraw from the Nephites."

## Conjunction and List Terms

### Polysyndeton
A list or series that uses repeated conjunctions between each item — "and X, and Y, and Z" — rather than commas. The repetition is deliberate, marking each item as a distinct rhetorical beat.
- **Colometric use:** Polysyndetic series with different registers stack vertically (Rule 20). Polysyndetic series in the same register may stay compressed.
- **Example (stack):** "and his matchless power, / and his wisdom, / and his patience, / and his long-suffering."
- **Example (compress):** "gold and silver and precious things" — same register.

### Asyndeton
A list or series with NO conjunctions between items — "humble, meek, submissive, patient." The items are comma-separated.
- **Colometric use:** Asyndetic lists in the same register compress on one line. A shift to a new register (the "pivot") earns a break.
- **Example:** "submissive, meek, humble, patient, / full of love." — the first four are asyndetic and compressed; "full of love" is the pivot.

### Merism
A pair using "both X and Y" to express a totality by naming its extremes — "both old and young," "both bond and free," "both male and female."
- **Colometric use:** Multiple merism pairs stack vertically, one pair per line.
- **Example:** "both old and young, / both bond and free, / both male and female."

### Dangling Conjunction
A line ending on "and," "or," "but," or "nor" with no content following on that line.
- **Colometric use:** Always wrong (Rule 9). Move the conjunction to lead the next line.

## Referential Terms

### Anaphoric
Pointing backward — a pronoun or demonstrative that refers to something already stated. "This," "that," "these things," "the case."
- **Colometric use:** Anaphoric "that" clauses with no new content merge (Rule 19): "that this should be the case" — both "this" and "the case" point back, no new image.

### Cataphoric
Pointing forward — a construction that creates anticipation for what comes next. FEFs are cataphoric by nature.
- **Colometric use:** Cataphoric "that" clauses with new content break (Rule 19): "that the good shepherd doth call you" — new image, new action.

### Expletive "It"
A structural placeholder "it" that doesn't refer to anything — used in cleft constructions like "it is by his grace that we have power."
- **Colometric use:** Expletive "it" in cleft constructions is cataphoric, not anaphoric. "That it is by his grace" introduces new content and can break.
- **Distinct from:** Referential "it" that points to a previously named thing ("that it should be the case").

## Adjective/Complement Terms

### Adjective + "That" Complement
A construction where an adjective requires a "that" clause to have meaning — "possible that," "expedient that," "desirous that," "necessary that."
- **Colometric use:** These stay together on one line (Rule 26). The adjective is incomplete without its complement, unlike verbs of speaking which are complete without their "that" clause.
- **Example:** "if it were possible that our first parents could have gone forth" — "possible" needs "that" to mean anything.

### Restrictive (Defining) Relative Clause
A relative clause that identifies WHICH one is meant — essential to the meaning. No commas in modern English.
- **Colometric use:** Thin restrictive clauses (extended adjectives) tend to merge: "the land which was called Bountiful." Substantial restrictive clauses with strong predication can break: "words which were delivered by the mouth of Abinadi."

### Non-Restrictive (Appositive) Relative Clause
A relative clause that adds supplementary information about an already-identified referent. Set off by commas in modern English.
- **Colometric use:** Non-restrictive clauses can break at "which/who" because the preceding line is already syntactically complete.
- **Note:** Punctuation (commas) must not be the sole basis for this distinction (punctuation principle). Test by grammar alone.

## Voice and Register Terms

### Register
The level or type of discourse — physical, spiritual, theological, legal, liturgical, emotional, etc. Two items in the "same register" are the same kind of thing.
- **Colometric use:** Items in the same register compress; a shift to a new register earns a break. This drives the escalatory appositive, polysyndetic stacking, and virtue list pivot decisions.
- **Example:** "humble, meek, submissive, patient" = same register (receptive humility). "Full of love" = new register (active outpouring). Break at the pivot.

### Mormonistic Style
Mormon's narrative voice as abridger/editor — characterized by high FEF density, "caused that" constructions, "and thus we see" editorial formulas, long irreducible frames, and compressed compound structures.
- **Colometric use:** Mormon's prose resists compression (short lines are rare). FEF frames are longer and heavier than in other voices.

### Sermonic/Oratorial Voice
The voice of embedded speakers (Alma, Benjamin, Abinadi, Christ) — characterized by shorter lines, rhetorical questions, "I say unto you" pauses, parallel stacking, vocative address.
- **Colometric use:** Sermonic text fragments more readily than narrative. The same foundational test applies, but the text generates more natural break points because the speaker is delivering discrete rhetorical punches.
